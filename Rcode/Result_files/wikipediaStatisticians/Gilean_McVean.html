Gilean Alistair Tristram McVean, FRS FMedSci is a professor of statistical genetics at the University of Oxford,[7] director of the Big Data Institute,[8] fellow of Linacre College, Oxford and co-founder and director at Genomics plc[9] He also co-chaired the 1000 Genomes Project analysis group.[10][11]

Education[edit]

From 1991 to 1994 McVean completed a BA in Biological Sciences at the University of Oxford.[12][13]

McVean completed his PhD in the Department of Genetics, at the University of Cambridge with Laurence Hurst[14][15] in 1997.[16]

Career and research[edit]

Following his PhD, McVean completed postdoctoral research at the University of Edinburgh from 1997 to 2000 under the supervision of Brian Charlesworth and Deborah Charlesworth.[17][18] From 2000-2004 he was a Royal Society University Research Fellow, in the Department of Statistics at Oxford, where he has also been a University lecturer in Mathematical Genetics since 2004 (reappointed in 2009 until retirement age).[19] In 2006 he was awarded a Philip Leverhulme Prize.[20][21] In October 2006 he became professor of statistical genetics at the University of Oxford.[22]

His research[23] focuses on population genetics, statistics[24] and evolutionary biology including the International HapMap Project,[25][26] recombination rates in the human genome[27] and the 1000 Genomes Project.[28][29] He developed a statistical method to look at recombination rate which helped to identify PRDM9 as a hotspot positioning gene.[30]

In 2014 with Peter Donnely he co-founded Genomics plc, a genomics analysis company, as a spin out of the University of Oxford.[31]

He was appointed as acting director of the new Big Data Institute at the University of Oxford.[8]

Honours and awards[edit]

In 2010 McVean was awarded the Francis Crick Medal and delivered that years lecture entitled "Our genomes, our history".[32]

In 2012 McVean was awarded the Weldon Memorial Prize.[33]

In 2013 McVean presented at TEDxWarwick with a talk entitled "A Thousand Genomes a Thousand Stories".[34]

In may of 2014 McVean was elected as a member of the European Molecular Biology Organisation.[35]

In 2016 McVean was elected Fellow of the Royal Society and fellow of the Academy of Medical Sciences.[36][37][38]
